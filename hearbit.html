Background

In a digital society, both algorithmic and human agents contribute to a transmission of knowledge. Humans often still have counterproductive human biases, which hold them back from reaching the optimal solution. One example of a counterproductive human bias is risk aversion: people favor a short term outcome over a long term outcome, which would have had a better reward. Because bots are able to produce novel solutions, they might help humans pass on better strategies in transmission chains.

Task

We conducted a large behavioral study and an agent-based simulation to test the performance of transmission chains with human and algorithmic players. In half of the chains, an algorithmic bot replaced a human participant.

My resposibilities

In this project, I helped out with data collection, data analysis and write up of the paper. I worked with Empirica and Prolific to conduct data of 20 groups, with 8 participants in each group. For the data analysis, I used R to model mostly linear mixed regression models and logistic regression models. For the write-up I contributed to the Introduction, Methods and Analysis.

Results

The results were counterintuitive to our hypothesis. Introducing bots to a social learning test in transmission chains did not improve the performance of the players in the long run. While humans copied from bots, they did so at a lower rate than copying humans with the same performance. We found, that Although we did not find the expected results this research sheds light to a new area of investigation which is currently relatively unexplored: hybrid social learning.
